Climate, Weather, and the Environment

Weather refers to short-term atmospheric conditions such as temperature, humidity, wind, and precipitation. Climate is the long-term average of weather patterns in a region, typically measured over 30 years.

The greenhouse effect is a natural process in which certain gases in Earth's atmosphere — including carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and water vapour — trap heat from the sun, keeping the planet warm enough to support life. Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be approximately -18°C instead of +15°C.

Human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas) and deforestation, have increased atmospheric CO2 concentrations from about 280 parts per million (ppm) before industrialisation to over 420 ppm today. This enhanced greenhouse effect is the primary driver of global warming.

Global average surface temperature has risen approximately 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels as of 2023, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). The IPCC's 1.5°C target, established in the Paris Agreement of 2015, aims to limit the most severe climate impacts.

Climate zones divide Earth into regions with similar climate characteristics: tropical (near the equator), arid/desert, temperate (mid-latitudes), continental, and polar (near the poles). Each zone supports distinct ecosystems and biodiversity.

Seasons occur because Earth's axis is tilted at 23.5° relative to its orbit. When the Northern Hemisphere tilts toward the sun, it experiences summer; when it tilts away, it experiences winter. The Southern Hemisphere's seasons are opposite to the Northern Hemisphere's.

Polar ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica contain about 99% of the world's freshwater ice. Their melting contributes to sea level rise, which threatens low-lying coastal areas. Current projections suggest global mean sea level could rise between 0.3 and 1 metre by 2100.

Extreme weather events — including heat waves, droughts, heavy rainfall, and intense hurricanes — are becoming more frequent and severe due to climate change.
