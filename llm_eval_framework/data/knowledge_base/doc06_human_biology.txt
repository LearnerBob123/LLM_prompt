Human Biology and Anatomy

The human body is composed of approximately 37 trillion cells, each performing specialised functions. Cells are the basic structural and functional units of life. Groups of similar cells form tissues; tissues form organs; organs form organ systems.

The circulatory system consists of the heart, blood vessels, and blood. The heart is a muscular organ that pumps blood through a network of arteries, veins, and capillaries. A healthy adult heart beats 60–100 times per minute and pumps about 5 litres of blood per minute. Blood carries oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products throughout the body.

The respiratory system includes the lungs, trachea, bronchi, and diaphragm. Oxygen from inhaled air passes through the alveoli (tiny air sacs) into the bloodstream, while carbon dioxide is expelled. An adult breathes approximately 12–20 times per minute at rest.

The digestive system breaks food down into nutrients. It begins at the mouth and includes the oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine. The small intestine is the primary site of nutrient absorption; the large intestine absorbs water and forms faeces.

The nervous system comprises the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. The brain has roughly 86 billion neurons. Neurons communicate via electrical signals (action potentials) and chemical messengers (neurotransmitters) across synapses.

The brain is divided into regions with specialised functions: the cerebral cortex handles thinking, language, and voluntary movement; the cerebellum coordinates balance and motor skills; the brainstem controls basic functions such as breathing and heart rate.

The skeletal system provides structure and protection. Adults have 206 bones. Bones store calcium and phosphorus, produce blood cells in the marrow, and anchor muscles via tendons.

The immune system defends the body against pathogens. White blood cells, antibodies, and the lymphatic system work together to identify and destroy bacteria, viruses, and other threats. Vaccines train the immune system by exposing it to a harmless form of a pathogen.
